🌱 The Basics

Get comfortable with your gear before jumping into specific shots.

Your setup

The EOS R50 V is a vlogging-focused mirrorless camera — flip-out screen, subject tracking autofocus, and a built-in mic. The RF 50mm f/1.8 is a small, sharp "nifty fifty." On your camera's APS-C sensor it acts like an ~80mm lens (a 1.6x crop), so it's flattering and tight for portraits/interviews, but too zoomed-in for full rooms or wide establishing shots. Keep that in mind — it'll come up a lot below.

The exposure triangle

  • Aperture (f-stop): controls how much background blur you get. f/1.8 = very blurry background, f/4–5.6 = more in focus.
  • Shutter speed: controls motion blur. For video, follow the 180-degree rule — shutter ≈ double your frame rate. 24fps → 1/50, 30fps → 1/60. Keep it here for natural-looking motion.
  • ISO: controls brightness/noise. Keep it as low as the light allows — ideally under 800 indoors, 100 outdoors.

Frame rate

24fps gives that classic cinematic feel. 30fps looks a little more "live" and smooth — good for real estate walkthroughs. Pick one per project and stick with it.

Autofocus

Use Movie Servo AF with Face/Eye Detect turned on — it'll track a person's eyes automatically, which is huge for interviews and run-and-gun.

Starter gear list

  • Fast SD card (V30 or better) — needed for smooth 4K recording
  • Spare battery — video eats battery fast
  • Variable ND filter (sized to the RF 50mm's filter thread) — lets you keep a wide aperture in bright light
  • External microphone (shotgun or lav) — built-in mic picks up handling noise and room echo
  • Mini tripod or small gimbal — smoothness matters more than almost anything else
  • Down the road: a wider lens (like an RF-S 10–18mm or RF 16mm) — the 50mm is too tight for interiors and real estate, so this will open up a lot more shot types

🛋️ Indoor Interviews

Sit-down, one or two people talking to camera or an off-screen interviewer.

Settings

  • Aperture: f/1.8–f/2.8 for a soft, blurred background (bump to f/2.8 if two people need to stay in focus)
  • Shutter: 1/50 at 24fps, or 1/60 at 30fps
  • ISO: as low as the room allows, ideally 100–800
  • White balance: match the room — Tungsten (~3200K) for warm lamps, Daylight (~5600K) near windows, or set a custom Kelvin value so it doesn't drift
  • Autofocus: Movie Servo AF + Face/Eye Detect, continuous

Gear & tips

  • The RF 50mm's tight framing (~80mm equivalent) is genuinely great here — very flattering for a single subject. Just make sure you have enough room to back up in a small space.
  • An external mic is almost required indoors — rooms echo and the built-in mic will pick it up.
  • Simple 3-point lighting (key light, fill light, and a small backlight to separate the subject from the background) makes a huge difference, even with cheap lights.

🌤️ Outdoor Interviews

Sit-down or standing interviews shot outside, in natural light.

Settings

  • Aperture: f/1.8–f/2.8 for background blur
  • Shutter: stay at 1/50 (24fps) or 1/60 (30fps) — don't raise it just because it's bright, use an ND filter instead
  • ISO: base ISO (100)
  • White balance: Daylight or Auto
  • Best light: open shade or golden hour (early morning/late afternoon) avoids harsh shadows and squinting; direct midday sun is the hardest to work with

Gear & tips

  • A variable ND filter is close to mandatory outdoors in daylight — without one you'll be forced to shoot at f/11+ just to avoid overexposing, which kills the background blur
  • Put the sun behind the camera, or behind the subject with a reflector/small LED bouncing light back onto their face
  • Add a foam windscreen ("dead cat") to your microphone outside — wind noise ruins outdoor audio fast

🎬 Cinematic B-Roll

The establishing shots, close-ups, and slow detail shots that make a video feel polished.

Settings

  • Aperture: f/1.8–f/2.8 for that shallow, dreamy depth of field
  • Frame rate: 24fps for the classic cinematic look
  • Shutter: 1/50, per the 180-degree rule
  • Movement: slow and deliberate — a slow push-in or gentle pan reads as "cinematic," fast movement just reads as shaky

Gear & tips

  • Even a cheap slider or gimbal elevates b-roll dramatically more than any camera setting — smooth, slow motion is the whole game here
  • The RF 50mm is actually excellent for close-up detail shots (hands, objects, small details) thanks to its close focus distance — lean into that
  • For wide establishing shots (a whole room, a landscape), the 50mm will feel too zoomed-in; that's a good use case for a wider lens down the road
  • Mix shot sizes on purpose: one wide/establishing shot, a few medium shots, and a couple of extreme close-ups per scene

🏃 Handheld Run & Gun

Fast-moving, no-tripod shooting — vlogging, events, following a subject around.

Settings

  • Stabilization: turn on Digital IS / stabilization in the video menu — this matters more than any other setting here
  • Aperture: f/2.8–f/4 — a little narrower than usual so focus is more forgiving while you're moving
  • Shutter: stay close to the 180-degree rule (1/50 or 1/60) when you can, but don't stress over it if the moment needs a faster shutter to stay sharp
  • ISO: don't be afraid to raise it — a little noise beats a blurry, unusable shot
  • Autofocus: Movie Servo AF with Whole Area or Zone tracking so it can grab a subject as they move through frame

Gear & tips

  • A wrist strap or small handheld rig/cage makes a big difference in stability and reduces fatigue
  • Keep a variable ND filter set to manual (not auto) — auto ND can visibly shift exposure at the worst moment when you walk from indoors to outdoors
  • Save your favorite settings to one of the camera's custom shooting modes so you can jump straight into run-and-gun mode without digging through menus
  • The RF 50mm's tight field of view makes run-and-gun harder in small spaces — a wider lens is one of the highest-value upgrades for this shot type specifically

🏡 Real Estate / Walkthrough Videos

Smooth room-to-room tours — different goals than portrait-style shots.

Settings

  • Aperture: f/4–f/5.6 — unlike interviews, you generally want the whole room in focus, not a blurred background
  • Frame rate: 24fps for cinematic, or 30fps for a smoother, more "real" feeling walkthrough
  • ISO: base ISO, use lighting/window light instead of raising it
  • White balance: set manually per room — mixed lighting (daylight through windows + warm lamps) can cause ugly color shifts if left on Auto
  • Best light: shoot during the day, ideally mid-morning to late afternoon, for the most natural-looking interiors

Gear & tips

  • A wide-angle lens is close to essential for this category — the RF 50mm (~80mm equivalent) is far too tight to capture a full room. This is the single best reason to add a wider lens.
  • A gimbal is what makes walkthroughs look professional: "slow is smooth, smooth is cinematic" — fast movement makes rooms feel smaller and cheaper
  • Use a tripod for static establishing shots of each room, then a gimbal for the moving walkthrough shots
  • Turn off or unplug lamps with a noticeably different color temperature than the rest of the room before shooting
